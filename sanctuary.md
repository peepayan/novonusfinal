*Purpose: Reference for writing Novonus copy — industrial robotics, force-aware AI, precision manufacturing*

## 1. HEADLINE PATTERNS

### Structure
Headlines are almost exclusively **noun-phrase or past-participle constructions** — not verb-led imperatives. They stack adjectives in front of the core noun to do the heavy lifting. A secondary pattern is the short gerund phrase used as a section title label.

| Pattern Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Adjective-noun stack | "Production-Ready Physical AI for Industrial Work" |
| Past participle | "Optimized for Dexterous and Dynamic Work" |
| Past participle | "Led by a Team of Industry Pioneers" |
| Noun phrase (compressed) | "High-Performance Policies for Challenging Industrial Tasks" |
| Gerund section label | "Solving Critical Challenges" |
| Gerund section label | "Automating What's Next" |
| Simple article + noun | "A Company on a Mission" |
| Imperative (rare — only the tagline) | "Automate What's Next" |

### Word Count
Average 5–6 words. Range: 2 words ("World-Class Team") to 8 words ("Led by a Team of Industry Pioneers"). The company's tagline is 3 words. Body-section headers run 3 words.

### Verbatim Headline Examples
- "Production-Ready Physical AI for Industrial Work"
- "Trusted Industrial Partner in Deploying Physical AI"
- "Optimized for Dexterous and Dynamic Work"
- "High-Performance Policies for Challenging Industrial Tasks"
- "Led by a Team of Industry Pioneers"
- "A Company on a Mission"
- "Preparing for a Future with Industrial Humanoids"
- "Trusted Industrial Brands with Custom End Effectors"
- "Proprietary Hydraulic Hands"
- "Mobility Platforms with AMRs"
- "Our Valued Funding Partners"
- "Automate What's Next" *(the company's master tagline/navigation anchor)*

### Key Observation
The word **"industrial"** appears in nearly every headline. It is the anchor modifier. Sanctuary does not write headlines for a general audience — every headline signals domain specificity immediately.

## 2. BODY COPY PATTERNS

### Sentence Length and Rhythm
The dominant pattern is **medium sentences (18–28 words)** built as a single clean clause. No run-ons. When a shorter punchy sentence appears, it opens the paragraph and is followed by a longer elaborating sentence:

> "Performance shapes everything we do. From our autonomous task policies to our supported hardware, we have set our bar high in order to meet the standards of top-tier companies around the world."

Short sentence asserts. Long sentence explains. This two-beat rhythm repeats throughout.

### Voice
**Primarily active voice.** Passive constructions are avoided in body copy; they appear only in headlines ("Optimized for...", "Led by...").

### Person
Sanctuary uses **both third person (company name) and first person ("we")** — context-dependent:
- Third person "Sanctuary AI" is used for **formal external claims** and introductory positioning: *"Sanctuary AI helps industrial leaders automate complex tasks..."*
- First person "we" is used for **direct product/capability statements**: *"We are creating the world's first industrial-grade robotic hand..."*, *"We partner with OEMs..."*, *"We deliver AI-powered robotic automation..."*
- The switch is clean — no mixing within a sentence.

### Verbatim Body Copy Examples
- "Sanctuary AI helps industrial leaders automate complex tasks by deploying Physical AI with production-ready performance across existing and future robotic hardware."
- "For industrial leaders who need a labor solution, Sanctuary AI deploys Physical AI on commercially available robotic systems to deliver production-ready performance on tasks traditional automation can't reliably handle."
- "Our Physical AI is built for multi-embodiment, supporting diverse hardware configurations, mobility options and end effectors, with a path to humanoids."
- "Performance shapes everything we do. From our autonomous task policies to our supported hardware, we have set our bar high in order to meet the standards of top-tier companies around the world."
- "Our team of global experts and industry leaders has been built with one thing in mind: mission success."
- "The company is expanding what automation can achieve today while preparing customers for the next generation of industrial dexterous technologies."
- "Headquartered in Vancouver, Canada, Sanctuary AI is recognized as a national hub for machine learning, robotics, and Physical AI talent."
- "See how Sanctuary AI is turning vision into reality through a growing track record of technological milestones."
- "Since 2018 Sanctuary AI has been trailblazing robotic dexterity. We are creating the world's first industrial-grade robotic hand that features human-level capabilities for industrial arm and humanoid integration."
- "We are training autonomous mobile robots in our lab to support deployment of intelligent systems capable of mobile manipulation. By adding mobility to our offering, we can automate a broader range of industrial work."
- "Our Physical AI will power the next generation of humanoid robots, enabling commercial-ready humanoids to deliver real work in the real industrial world."
- "Trusted by leading global investors, Sanctuary AI is shaping the future of Physical AI and robotic innovation."

## 3. TECHNICAL LANGUAGE

### Primary Brand Term
**"Physical AI"** — always capitalized as a proper two-word brand noun. This is Sanctuary's core category label. They do not say "AI-powered robots" or "AI software." Everything is Physical AI. It appears in nearly every paragraph.

### Technical Terms Used (and How)
| Term | How It Appears |
|---|---|
| Physical AI | Core brand noun, capitalized always, used relentlessly |
| production-ready | Hyphenated modifier, used constantly — the quality claim |
| dexterous / dexterity | Key differentiator; "dexterous work," "dexterous technologies," "dexterous policies" |
| multi-embodiment | Their flexibility claim — runs on multiple hardware types |
| autonomous task policies | Technical but used without definition |
| end effectors | Used without explanation — assumes B2B industrial reader |
| sim-to-real transfer | Blog/news context; not front-page body copy |
| reinforcement learning | Blog/news context |
| zero-shot in-hand manipulation | Blog/news context |
| high degree-of-freedom | Descriptive spec language for the hand |
| uni- or bi-manual systems | Configuration options, technical |
| mobile manipulation | Mobility + dexterity combined use case |
| intelligent automation | Used once; not their core label |
| AMRs | Acronym used for Autonomous Mobile Robots without definition |
| OEMs, Tier 1 and 2 suppliers | Industry-standard automotive shorthand, no explanation |

### Terms They Avoid
- "AI-powered" (generic; they own "Physical AI" instead)
- "AGI," "general intelligence," "superintelligence" (no overclaims)
- "robot" as a standalone brand noun (always qualified: "industrial robot," "humanoid robot," "general purpose robot")
- "smart" (too casual)
- "cutting-edge," "state-of-the-art" (clichés)

### How They Describe the Product to Non-Experts
They do not define Physical AI explicitly on the homepage — they demonstrate it through outcomes. The framing is always: *what it runs on* (commercially available robots), *what it does* (automates complex tasks), *how well* (production-ready, 99.5%+ success rate). They anchor abstraction with the word "today" — present tense credibility.

### Exact Product Description (Verbatim)
> "Sanctuary AI powers the world's most trusted industrial robots with advanced Physical AI to deliver intelligent automation. Configure custom end effectors and uni- or bi-manual systems to fit your needs."

> "We are creating the world's first industrial-grade robotic hand that features human-level capabilities for industrial arm and humanoid integration."

## 4. TONE DESCRIPTORS

### Primary Tone: Calm Authority
No exclamation marks. No hyperbolic adjectives. No urgency pressure tactics. The register is closer to a defense contractor briefing than a startup pitch deck. Claims are large ("world's most trusted," "world's first industrial-grade robotic hand") but delivered flatly.

### Confidence Without Bravado
Sanctuary makes strong claims and does not hedge them with qualifiers like "we believe" or "we hope." But they also don't shout. The stat "99.5%+ SUCCESS RATE" appears as a data point, not a victory lap.

### Human-Warm vs Cold-Technical
Slightly on the **warmer side of technical**. The phrase "mission success" and the framing around workers doing "more empowering work" signal human concern. But warmth is never gushy — it reads as principled rather than emotional.

### Aspirational vs Present-Capability
Carefully **dual-tracked**: present capability is stated as fact ("Now Deploying," "production-ready performance"); future is explicitly flagged as future ("In Development," "Piloting Soon," "path to humanoids," "Preparing for a Future with..."). They never conflate the two.

### B2B Formal vs Startup Casual
Firmly **B2B formal**. The customer is always "industrial leaders," never "teams" or "companies" or "you guys." The language assumes procurement cycles, not impulse decisions.

## 5. GRAMMAR & STYLE RULES

### Capitalization
- **"Physical AI"** — always Title Case as a brand noun
- **Page headlines** — Title Case throughout (*"Production-Ready Physical AI for Industrial Work"*)
- **Status labels and CTAs** — ALL CAPS (*"NOW DEPLOYING," "IN DEVELOPMENT," "CONTACT COMMERCIAL TEAM," "PHYSICAL AI EMBODIMENT ROADMAP"*)
- **Section anchors in navigation** — ALL CAPS (*"SOLUTIONS"*)
- **Section content sub-labels** — Sentence case (*"Solving Critical Challenges," "Automating What's Next"*)

### Oxford Comma
**Yes.** Confirmed: *"machine learning, robotics, and Physical AI talent."*

### Em Dashes
Not present in the scraped body copy. Colons are used instead to signal elaboration:
> "Our team of global experts and industry leaders has been built with one thing in mind: mission success."

### Sentence Fragments
Used deliberately as **section label headings**: "World-Class Team," "Innovation Track Record," "Solving Critical Challenges." These are noun/gerund phrases, not full sentences. Body copy is always complete sentences.

### Numbers
- Stats: Numeric with symbol — "99.5%+"
- Years: Numeric — "Since 2018"
- Ordinals: Spelled out — "the eighth generation," "the next generation," "world's first"
- No numbered lists in body copy — prose only

### Hyphenation
Heavy use of compound modifiers before nouns:
- production-ready
- world-class
- high-performance
- industrial-grade
- multi-embodiment
- bi-manual / uni- or bi-manual
- zero-shot
- sim-to-real

### Punctuation Mood
No exclamation points anywhere. Periods close even fragments used as headlines (*"To deliver production-ready Physical AI to industry today."* — note: this is a sentence fragment, but still closed with a period).

## 7. MISSION & VALUES LANGUAGE

### Exact Mission Statement
> "To deliver production-ready Physical AI to industry today."

Note: "today" is the operative word — grounding the mission in the present, not a distant future. This is deliberate positioning against competitors who overpromise.

### How They Talk About Workers and Humans
Sanctuary is careful to frame automation as **labor augmentation, not replacement**. The phrase "dull, dirty, and dexterous work" appears in their content, positioning automation as taking the undesirable tasks so that workers can do "more empowering work." This is the human-side justification.

They refer to their customer as:
- "industrial leaders" (always — not "manufacturers," "companies," "clients")
- "OEMs and Tier 1 and 2 suppliers" (in automotive context)

### How They Talk About Industry
The company language treats industry with respect — it is not a problem to be disrupted but a domain to be served. Phrases like "meet the standards of top-tier companies around the world" position the customer as the authority, not Sanctuary.

### Values-Adjacent Language (Verbatim)
- "Our team of global experts and industry leaders has been built with one thing in mind: mission success."
- "See how Sanctuary AI is turning vision into reality through a growing track record of technological milestones."
- "Canada's Physical AI Moment Can't Wait" *(thought leadership headline — urgency, national pride)*
- "Trusted by leading global investors, Sanctuary AI is shaping the future of Physical AI and robotic innovation."

## 8. WHAT TO AVOID

### Clichés Sanctuary Never Uses
- "revolutionize" / "revolutionizing"
- "disrupt" / "disrupting the industry"
- "game-changing"
- "next-level"
- "seamless"
- "unlock" (as in "unlock your potential")
- "cutting-edge"
- "state-of-the-art"
- "world-class" used in body copy (only in headlines; never in running prose)
- "leverage" as a verb for business strategy

### Startup Buzzwords Not Present
- "democratize"
- "ecosystem"
- "synergy" / "synergies"
- "pivot"
- "scale" used as a standalone verb ("we scale")
- "AI-powered" (they own Physical AI instead)
- "end-to-end" (common automation cliché)
- "plug-and-play"

### Tone Patterns to Avoid
- Exclamation points
- First-person singular "I" anywhere
- Founder-voice ("We started this company because we believed...")
- Overpromising on AGI or general-purpose intelligence timelines
- Framing the customer as behind or failing ("Are you still using...?")
- Consumer-facing warmth (emojis, casual contractions like "you're gonna love")

## 9. APPLICATION NOTES FOR NOVONUS

Sanctuary AI is the **closest competitive comps for tone and language**. Key takeaways when writing Novonus copy:

1. **Anchor every claim to present performance.** Use "today," "now," "production-ready" equivalents. Novonus should feel deployable right now.
2. **"Force-aware AI" should be treated like Sanctuary treats "Physical AI"** — capitalize it, repeat it, own it as a category label.
3. **Name the customer precisely.** "Precision manufacturers" or "manufacturing engineers" is the equivalent of Sanctuary's "industrial leaders."
4. **Lead headlines with the outcome, not the technology.** Sanctuary says "Production-Ready Physical AI for Industrial Work" — not "Our Advanced AI Platform."
5. **Use numeric performance claims flatly.** "99.5%+ success rate" is delivered without exclamation. Novonus force/torque specs, rejection rates, cycle time improvements should be delivered the same way.
6. **The two-beat sentence rhythm works.** Short declarative → longer elaborating sentence. Use it.
7. **Keep CTAs functional.** "Contact Our Engineering Team" or "Explore Force Applications" — not "Get Started" or "Book a Demo."
8. **Avoid "smart," "intelligent," "AI-powered."** Sanctuary avoids these too. Specificity earns more trust than adjectives.
9. **Hyphenate compound modifiers aggressively.** force-aware, precision-rated, sub-millimeter, high-fidelity.
10. **Workers deserve better framing.** Follow Sanctuary's lead: automation takes the imprecise, fatiguing, high-variance tasks so that skilled workers do higher-value work.
